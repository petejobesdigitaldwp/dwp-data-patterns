Methodology

Where do the numbers in "Manhattan" come from?

Data source

We source the data for "Manhattan" from Her Majesty's Revenue and Customs (HMRC). It represens data extracted from P45/P46 and P14 records. These summarise a person's employment taken from three points. When they end a job, start a job, or every year during someone's employment. At source, the data covers the tax year from April 2015 to March 2016. It lists each employment spell — a person may have more than one job at once — as a record.

Each record has information on start dates and end dates. It also has the gross pay to that individual, by that employer, over the year.

As this data is about employers, and belongs to another government department, it is handled carefully by DWP. It is subject to a memorandum of understanding (MoU) between HMRC and DWP. This MoU controls how we can use the data.

Employee counts

Start of year counts

We derive these aggregated statistics from the HMRC data.

We count the number of employees who lived in a 2015 Local Authority District, and who worked for a given company, in April 2015.

End of year countsexperimental

We also count again, after a measured change of employees through the year.

This data is incomplete, so we project the counts. We do this by matching with published national statistics to make an estimate.

This produces an estimated count for March 2016. This projection gives us more accurate trends, but individual counts may not be accurate.

Average salaries

We derive these aggregated statistics from the HMRC data.

It is a weighted mean of individual gross pay for employees who lived in a given 2015 Local Authority District who worked for a given company in April 2015.

We chose weighted means, over the more standard median measure. This is to allow us to combine aggregated stats in a consistent way. Unfortunately, as a result, outliers have a greater affect on the data.

To lessen this effect, we calculate weighted means from medians of a lower level geography, instead of a local authority. We then use these to calculate the numbers for local authorities.

Changes experimental

We derive these statistics from the HMRC data, as well as published statistics on employment.

We compare the distributions of the number of employees at the start of the year (April 2015) to national statistics. We then do the same for the end of the year (March 2016), which is incomplete.

Next, we use the relationship between the distributions at the start of the year to adjust the values for the end of the year. This then lets us estimate a change between the start and end. We also calculate a percentage change by dividing the change by the employee count at the start of the year.

Employer extents experimental

We derive these statistics from the HMRC data.

These describe how widespread an employer's footprint is across the country in plain English.

We do this by using information entropy. Information entropy is highest when the employee count for an employer is the same in every local authority in the country. It is lowest when the employee count for an employer is zero in all local authorities in the country, except one.

The top one percent of entropy values are considered to represent "national" employers.

The top ten percent of entropy values are considered to represent "regional" employers.

The bottom ninety percent of entropy values are considered to represent "local" employers.

We picked these thresholds through trial and error.
